How to avoid the trap of automatic thinking and anger

Andrea Piacquadio/pexels

A new article explains how automatic thinking can lead to anger and how to overcome it.

What is automatic thinking and how does it affect anger?

Automatic thinking is the process of having thoughts that are spontaneous, involuntary, and often unconscious. These thoughts are influenced by our beliefs, assumptions, expectations, and emotions. Automatic thinking can be positive or negative, depending on the situation and our interpretation of it.

Automatic thinking can affect our anger in two ways. First, it can trigger anger by making us perceive a situation as threatening, unfair, or frustrating. For example, if someone cuts us off in traffic, we may have an automatic thought like "He is so rude and selfish!" This thought can make us feel angry and resentful toward the other driver.

Second, it can maintain or intensify anger by making us focus on the negative aspects of a situation and ignore the positive ones. For example, if we have an argument with our partner, we may have automatic thoughts like "He never listens to me!" or "She always criticizes me!" These thoughts can make us feel more angry and hurt by our partner's behavior.

How to overcome automatic thinking and anger

Automatic thinking can be helpful in some situations, such as when we need to make quick decisions or react to emergencies. However, when it comes to anger, automatic thinking can be harmful and irrational. It can make us overreact, say or do things we regret, damage our relationships, and affect our health and well-being.

Therefore, it is important to learn how to overcome automatic thinking and anger by using some strategies, such as:

  • Recognizing automatic thoughts: The first step is to become aware of our automatic thoughts and how they affect our anger. We can do this by paying attention to our inner dialogue and noticing what we say to ourselves when we feel angry. We can also write down our automatic thoughts and the situations that trigger them.
  • Challenging automatic thoughts: The second step is to question the validity and accuracy of our automatic thoughts. We can do this by asking ourselves some questions, such as: Is this thought based on facts or feelings? Is this thought realistic or exaggerated? Is this thought helpful or harmful? What evidence do I have to support or refute this thought? What are some alternative ways of looking at this situation?
  • Replacing automatic thoughts: The third step is to replace our negative automatic thoughts with more positive and rational ones. We can do this by using some techniques, such as: Reframing the situation from a different perspective. For example, instead of thinking "He is so rude and selfish!" we can think "Maybe he is in a hurry or having a bad day." Finding the positive aspects of the situation. For example, instead of thinking "He never listens to me!" we can think "He listens to me sometimes and he cares about me." Using coping statements that help us calm down and cope with anger. For example, instead of thinking "She always criticizes me!" we can think "Her criticism is not about me, it's about her own issues." or "I can handle this situation without losing my temper."
