How emotional stability affects romantic relationships: A new study reveals the key factor for lasting love

cottonbro studio/pexels

A new study from Taiwan suggests that emotional stability, rather than personality compatibility or similarity, is the most important predictor of relationship satisfaction and longevity.

The study: Emotional stability matters more than personality traits

The study, published in the Journal of Research in Personality, involved 1,523 heterosexual couples who completed online surveys about their personality traits, emotional fluctuations, relationship satisfaction and commitment.

The researchers used a well-known personality model called the Big Five, which measures five broad dimensions of personality: openness, conscientiousness, extraversion, agreeableness and neuroticism.

They also measured emotional fluctuations, which refer to how often and how intensely people experience positive and negative emotions.

The results showed that emotional fluctuations were negatively associated with relationship satisfaction and commitment, meaning that people who had more stable emotions were happier and more loyal to their partners.

On the other hand, personality traits had little or no direct effect on relationship outcomes, except for conscientiousness, which was positively related to commitment.

The researchers also found that emotional fluctuations mediated the effects of personality traits on relationship outcomes, meaning that personality traits influenced relationship satisfaction and commitment through their impact on emotional stability.

For example, people who were more neurotic tended to have more emotional fluctuations, which in turn lowered their relationship satisfaction and commitment.

Similarly, people who were more agreeable tended to have less emotional fluctuations, which in turn increased their relationship satisfaction and commitment.

The implications: Focus on managing emotions rather than finding the perfect match

The findings suggest that emotional stability is a key factor for maintaining a healthy and lasting romantic relationship, regardless of personality compatibility or similarity.

The researchers explained that emotional fluctuations can undermine relationship quality by creating uncertainty, conflict and stress for both partners.

They also noted that emotional stability can enhance relationship quality by fostering trust, intimacy and support.

Therefore, they advised couples to focus on managing their emotions rather than finding the perfect match based on personality traits.

They suggested that couples can improve their emotional stability by practicing mindfulness, gratitude, optimism and self-compassion.

They also recommended that couples seek professional help if they struggle with chronic or severe emotional problems that affect their relationship.

The context: Previous research on personality and relationships

Previous research on personality and relationships has yielded mixed results. Some studies have found that personality compatibility or similarity can predict relationship satisfaction and longevity, while others have found no or weak effects.

Some of the factors that may account for these inconsistencies include the use of different personality models, measures and methods; the influence of other variables such as age, gender and culture; and the complexity and dynamics of human relationships.

The current study adds to the existing literature by using a large and diverse sample of couples; a comprehensive and validated measure of personality; a novel measure of emotional fluctuations; and sophisticated statistical analyses.

However, the study also has some limitations, such as the reliance on self-reported data; the cross-sectional design that does not capture changes over time; and the lack of information on other aspects of relationship quality such as communication, intimacy and conflict.

Therefore, future research is needed to replicate and extend the findings using longitudinal, experimental and observational methods; incorporating other measures of relationship quality; and examining potential moderators and mediators of the effects of personality and emotional fluctuations on relationship outcomes.
