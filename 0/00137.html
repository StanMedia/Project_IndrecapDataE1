Brazil probes soccer match-fixing involving top clubs and players

Stanley Morales/pexels

The federal police have launched an investigation into allegations that athletes from major Brazilian soccer clubs collaborated with gangs that manipulated sports betting.

The investigation follows a state-level probe that accused 16 people of rigging 13 matches

The Brazilian Ministry of Justice and Public Security announced on Wednesday that it had ordered the federal police to open an inquiry into soccer match-fixing, after the disclosure that athletes from main division clubs of Brazilian soccer were suspected of collaborating with gangs that would act through sports betting.

The decision came hours after newspaper O Globo reported that Brazilian midfielder Max Alves of the Colorado Rapids, a Major League Soccer club in the United States, had been named in the probe.

According to O Globo, Alves was one of the players who received money from a gang leader to influence the outcome of matches in Brazil's Serie A and Serie B, as well as the state championship in 2022 and 2023.

The newspaper said Alves was paid $100,000 (500.000 Brazilian Reais) for a red card in a match between his former club Goias and Internacional in 2022. The newspaper also said Alves confessed his involvement to the state prosecutors and cooperated with the investigation.

The federal investigation follows a state-level probe by the Goias Public Prosecutor's office, which accused 16 people of manipulating the results of 13 soccer matches in Brazil. The state prosecutors said they had evidence of phone calls, bank transfers and testimonies that implicated the suspects.

The state prosecutors said the gang leader, who was arrested on Tuesday, had contacts with players, coaches, referees and agents who helped him rig the matches. The gang leader allegedly made millions of dollars by betting on the fixed matches through online platforms based in Asia and Europe.

The soccer clubs and players have denied any wrongdoing or involvement

Some of the Brazilian soccer clubs whose players were allegedly involved in the match-fixing scheme have issued statements denying any wrongdoing or involvement.

Goias, which had four players named in the probe, said it had suspended them pre-emptively and was cooperating with the authorities. The club also said it was a victim of the scheme and had suffered losses due to the manipulated results.

Internacional, which had two players named in the probe, said it had no knowledge of any irregularities and was awaiting more information from the authorities. The club also said it trusted its players and staff and supported their right to defend themselves.

Flamengo, which had one player named in the probe, said it had not been notified by any authority and was surprised by the allegations. The club also said it supported its player and believed in his innocence.

The Colorado Rapids also confirmed that one of their players was connected to the Brazilian match-fixing scandal and said they had removed him from all team activities while MLS conducted an investigation into the matter.

MLS also said one former player was involved in "unlawful sports gambling" and said it took seriously these allegations and any contravention of the league's integrity rule.

The Brazilian Football Confederation (CBF) said it was following the case closely and was ready to collaborate with any investigation. The CBF also said it had a strict code of ethics and conduct that applied to all participants of its competitions.

The match-fixing scandal has tarnished the reputation of Brazilian soccer

The match-fixing scandal has tarnished the reputation of Brazilian soccer, which is already struggling with financial difficulties, corruption scandals and poor performance on the international stage.

Brazilian soccer fans have expressed their outrage and disappointment on social media, calling for harsh punishments for those involved and reforms to improve the transparency and governance of the sport.

Some analysts have also warned that the match-fixing scandal could have negative consequences for Brazil's bid to host the 2030 FIFA World Cup, which it is competing with Argentina, Uruguay and Paraguay.

Brazil is widely regarded as one of the most successful and influential countries in soccer history, having won five World Cups and produced some of the greatest players of all time. However, its soccer industry has also been plagued by problems such as violence, racism, sexism and mismanagement.
