A new nanomaterial inspired by mussels could help tackle textile wastewater pollution

Andrew Martin/Pixabay

Researchers in Abu Dhabi have developed a novel material that can remove harmful dyes from industrial wastewater

The problem of textile wastewater

Every year, the textile industry uses 1.3 trillion gallons of water to dye garments – enough to fill 2 million Olympic-sized swimming pools. Most of this water, loaded with harmful chemicals and dyes, flows untreated into rivers and streams. This not only affects the aquatic life and ecosystems, but also poses serious health risks to humans who depend on these water sources for drinking, irrigation, and sanitation.

The textile industry is one of the biggest contributors to industrial wastewater, accounting for up to 20% of the global total. However, it is not alone: manufacturing, mining, petrochemicals, pharmaceuticals and agriculture all add to the problem. The United Nations estimates that globally, nearly 95 trillion gallons of wastewater are produced every year – roughly equivalent to 41 years of drinking water for the entire human population.

A landmark report, published last month by the Global Commission on the Economics of Water (GCEW), found that just 20% of wastewater is currently treated, with far less being recycled, contributing to a global shortage of clean water. The report anticipates that demand for freshwater will outstrip supply by 40% by 2030.

The solution inspired by shellfish

To address this challenge, researchers at Khalifa University in Abu Dhabi, United Arab Emirates (UAE), have created a new nanomaterial that they say can clean up these dyes and other pollutants from industrial wastewater. The material consists of tiny sand-like grains, only visible to the naked-eye in clusters, which collect pollutants on their surfaces and in their pores.

The nanomaterial consists of a substance called a polymer that mimics the "glue" that mussels use to stick themselves to rocks, combined with a solvent. Finding the right solvent was a challenge, says Enas Nashef, project lead and a professor of chemical engineering at Khalifa University, because most solvents are toxic, but the team identified one which is both effective and, crucially, eco-friendly.

"If the thing you use will pollute the water, then what is the benefit?" he says.

The researchers took inspiration from the protein in mussels' "beards." These strong, sticky threads help the shellfish attach to rocks and reefs. The protein has a unique ability to form strong bonds with various surfaces in wet conditions. By mimicking this property, the researchers were able to create a polymer that can adhere to different types of dyes and pollutants.

The team tested their nanomaterial on a red-orange dye called Alizarin Red S, which is widely used in the textile industry and is known to be carcinogenic and mutagenic. They published their findings earlier this year in the journal ACS Applied Materials & Interfaces.

"So far, there are no toxic effects," says Nashef, adding that the polymer can be cleaned of the pollutants and then reused. "We’re looking at the efficiency and also the environment at the same time," he says.

The team claims that their nanomaterial can remove up to 99% of Alizarin Red S from water within 15 minutes. They also tested it on other types of dyes and pollutants, such as methylene blue and cadmium ions, with similar results.

They targeted "anionic dyes," because there are not many effective methods to remove these types of dye from water. Nashef’s hopes his high-efficiency material can make a dent in the sector’s dirty wastewater problem.

The potential impact and challenges

The researchers say that their nanomaterial has several advantages over existing methods of wastewater treatment. For instance, it does not require high temperatures or pressures, which can save energy and costs. It also does not generate any secondary waste or sludge, which can pose disposal problems.

Moreover, it can be easily scaled up for industrial applications. Nashef says that they have already patented their invention and are looking for partners to commercialize it. He envisions that their nanomaterial could be used in filters or reactors that can treat large volumes of wastewater at once.

However, there are also some challenges and limitations that need to be addressed before their nanomaterial can be widely adopted. For example, they need to test its performance and safety on more types of dyes and pollutants, as well as on real wastewater samples from different industries. They also need to optimize its synthesis process and reduce its cost.

Furthermore, they need to consider the social and economic factors that may influence the adoption of their nanomaterial in different regions and industries. For example, they need to consider the regulations, incentives, and awareness of wastewater treatment in different countries, as well as the costs and benefits of their technology compared to other alternatives.

The future of wastewater treatment

The researchers believe that their nanomaterial has the potential to make a significant impact on the textile wastewater problem, as well as on other types of industrial wastewater. They hope that their invention can help reduce water pollution and conserve freshwater resources, which are essential for human health and well-being.

They also hope that their work can inspire more research and innovation in the field of wastewater treatment, especially in developing novel materials and methods that are eco-friendly and efficient. They suggest that more collaboration between academia, industry, and government is needed to facilitate the development and implementation of such technologies.

They also emphasize the importance of educating the public and raising awareness about the environmental and social impacts of wastewater pollution, as well as the benefits of wastewater treatment and reuse. They believe that by changing the perception and behavior of consumers and producers, more sustainable practices can be adopted in the textile industry and beyond.
