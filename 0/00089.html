The Good Friday Agreement: How a historic deal ended the Troubles in Northern Ireland

David Dibert/Pexels

A look back at the 1998 peace accord that brought an end to three decades of sectarian violence and established a power-sharing government.

The background of the conflict

Northern Ireland was created in 1921 when Ireland was partitioned by the Government of Ireland Act 1920. It remained part of the United Kingdom while the rest of Ireland became an independent state. This created a division in the population between unionists, who wanted to stay within the UK, and nationalists, who wanted to join the Republic of Ireland.

From the late 1960s, Northern Ireland witnessed a period of violent conflict known as the Troubles, which involved paramilitary groups from both sides, such as the Irish Republican Army (IRA) and the Ulster Volunteer Force (UVF), as well as British security forces. The Troubles claimed more than 3,500 lives and injured tens of thousands more.

The conflict was fuelled by political, economic and social inequalities between the two communities, as well as by historical grievances and cultural differences. The unionists, who were mostly Protestant, dominated the state institutions and discriminated against the nationalists, who were mostly Catholic. The nationalists, on the other hand, felt alienated and oppressed by the British government and demanded civil rights and self-determination.

Several attempts to find a political solution to the conflict failed, such as the Sunningdale Agreement of 1973 and the Anglo-Irish Agreement of 1985. The violence continued throughout the 1970s and 1980s, with bombings, shootings and hunger strikes. In the early 1990s, however, a series of events paved the way for a new peace process.

The road to peace

In 1993, the British and Irish governments issued a joint declaration, known as the Downing Street Declaration, which affirmed their commitment to finding a peaceful and democratic settlement for Northern Ireland. The declaration also recognized the right of self-determination for the people of Northern Ireland and offered to include all parties in negotiations if they renounced violence.

In response to this declaration, the IRA announced a ceasefire in 1994, followed by a similar move by the loyalist paramilitaries. This created a window of opportunity for dialogue and diplomacy. In 1996, an international body chaired by former US Senator George Mitchell issued a report that outlined six principles for peace talks, such as democracy, non-violence and consent.

In 1997, after a brief breakdown of the ceasefire, a new British government led by Tony Blair and a new Irish government led by Bertie Ahern resumed the peace process. They invited all parties that accepted the Mitchell principles to participate in multi-party negotiations. These talks were held in Belfast and lasted for almost two years.

The content of the agreement

On April 10, 1998, after intense discussions and compromises, the parties reached an agreement that was later endorsed by referendums in both Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland. The agreement was also known as the Belfast Agreement or simply the Agreement.

The Agreement consisted of two interrelated documents: a multi-party agreement that dealt with internal matters in Northern Ireland; and an international agreement between Britain and Ireland that dealt with external matters.

The main features of the Agreement were:

  • A devolved power-sharing government for Northern Ireland, composed of representatives from both communities and accountable to a new elected assembly.
  • A cross-border body called the North/South Ministerial Council that would facilitate cooperation between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland on matters of mutual interest.
  • A British-Irish Council that would bring together representatives from Britain, Ireland, Northern Ireland, Scotland, Wales and other islands to discuss issues of common concern.
  • A British-Irish Intergovernmental Conference that would allow Britain and Ireland to consult on matters not devolved to Northern Ireland.
  • A reform of policing and justice in Northern Ireland to make them more accountable and representative of the community.
  • A decommissioning of paramilitary weapons under the supervision of an independent commission.
  • A release of prisoners affiliated with paramilitary groups that had declared ceasefires.
  • A recognition of the right of people born in Northern Ireland to hold either British or Irish citizenship or both.
  • A confirmation that Northern Ireland's constitutional status would only change if a majority of its people consented to it.

The challenges of implementation

The Agreement was not a magic bullet that solved all the problems in Northern Ireland overnight. It faced many obstacles and setbacks in its implementation, such as:

  • Disagreements over the timing and verification of decommissioning, which delayed the formation of the power-sharing government until 1999 and caused its suspension several times until 2007.
  • Disputes over contentious issues such as policing, justice, parades, flags and symbols, which often sparked protests and violence on the streets.
  • Dissident republican and loyalist groups that rejected the Agreement and continued to carry out attacks, such as the Omagh bombing in 1998 that killed 29 people and injured more than 200.
  • Political scandals and crises that eroded trust and cooperation between the parties, such as the Stormont spy ring in 2002, the Iris Robinson affair in 2010 and the Renewable Heat Incentive scandal in 2017.
  • The impact of Brexit on the status of Northern Ireland and its relationship with the Republic of Ireland, which raised fears of a hard border and a threat to the peace process.

Despite these challenges, the Agreement has also achieved significant progress and benefits for Northern Ireland, such as:

  • A reduction in violence and deaths related to the conflict, as well as an increase in security and stability.
  • A transformation of the political landscape, with former enemies working together in government and engaging in dialogue and compromise.
  • A growth in the economy, tourism and investment, as well as an improvement in living standards and social services.
  • A promotion of human rights, equality and diversity, as well as a recognition of different identities and cultures.
  • A development of cross-community and cross-border initiatives, projects and partnerships that fostered reconciliation and cooperation.

The legacy of the agreement

The Agreement is widely regarded as a landmark achievement that ended one of the longest-running conflicts in modern history and established a model for peacebuilding and conflict resolution. It has been praised by international leaders and organizations, such as former US President Bill Clinton, former UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan and former South African President Nelson Mandela.

The Agreement has also inspired other peace processes around the world, such as those in Colombia, Sri Lanka, Kosovo and the Middle East. It has been studied by academics, practitioners and students as a case study of successful negotiation, mediation and compromise.

However, the Agreement is not a static document that can be taken for granted. It is a dynamic process that requires constant monitoring, review and adaptation to changing circumstances and challenges. It also requires ongoing commitment, dialogue and goodwill from all parties involved, as well as support from external actors.

As former US Senator George Mitchell said: "The peace process is not an event; it is a long-term effort to create an environment in which people can live together peacefully."
