The rise of ChatGPT and the quest for mind-reading AI

Pavel Danilyuk/pexels

Scientists are using artificial intelligence models inspired by ChatGPT to decode brain activity and translate thoughts into text.

What is ChatGPT and how does it relate to mind-reading AI?

ChatGPT is a text-generating system created by OpenAI, a research organization backed by Elon Musk and other tech luminaries. It uses deep learning models, such as neural networks, to learn from large amounts of data and generate outputs that are similar but not identical to the inputs.

For example, ChatGPT can produce realistic-looking faces of people who do not exist, write coherent paragraphs of text on any topic, or compose original music based on a genre or style.

ChatGPT has been used for various purposes, such as writing fake news articles, creating viral memes, generating fake reviews, and even flirting with users. It has also sparked ethical and social concerns about the potential misuse and abuse of artificial intelligence for malicious or deceptive purposes.

ChatGPT has also inspired scientists who are interested in developing mind-reading AI, which is a type of artificial intelligence that can decode brain activity and translate thoughts into text or speech. This could have applications for people who have lost the ability to speak due to stroke, motor neurone disease or other conditions.

One of the challenges of mind-reading AI is that brain activity is noisy, sluggish and complex, making it difficult to interpret in real time. However, by using artificial intelligence models similar to ChatGPT, scientists can overcome this challenge by focusing on the meaning and semantics of thoughts rather than the exact words.

How does mind-reading AI work and what are the latest advances?

Mind-reading AI works by using a device that can measure brain activity, such as an fMRI scanner or an EEG headset. The device records the patterns of neuronal activity in different regions of the brain while the person listens to or thinks about something.

The recorded brain activity is then fed into an artificial intelligence model that has been trained to match brain activity to meaning using a large database of books and websites. The model then generates text that corresponds to the meaning of the thoughts.

One of the latest advances in mind-reading AI was reported by neuroscientists at the University of Texas at Austin, who used an artificial intelligence model based on GPT-1, a precursor to ChatGPT, to translate brain activity into text.

The researchers trained the model to analyze the brain activity of volunteers who listened to audio clips while lying in an fMRI scanner. The model learned how sentences are constructed and how words relate to each other. The model was then able to predict what the volunteers were listening to or thinking about just by watching their brain activity.

The researchers said their system was able to reconstruct speech with uncanny accuracy while people listened to a story – or even silently imagined one – using only fMRI scan data. They said their system could potentially be used to help people who have lost the ability to speak communicate with others.

What are the challenges and opportunities for mind-reading AI?

Mind-reading AI is still in its early stages and faces many challenges and limitations. For instance, it requires expensive and invasive devices that are not widely available or accessible. It also depends on the quality and quantity of data that is used to train the artificial intelligence models. It also raises ethical and privacy issues about who can access and use the thoughts of others.

However, mind-reading AI also offers many opportunities and benefits for society and humanity. For example, it could provide new ways of communication and expression for people who cannot speak or write. It could also enhance education and entertainment by allowing people to learn from or enjoy stories directly from their minds. It could also advance scientific understanding of how the brain works and how thoughts are formed.
