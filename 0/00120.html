Australia cracks down on e-cigarettes amid rising teen vaping

MART PRODUCTION/pexels

The government announces a ban on recreational vaping and new regulations on nicotine products to curb youth addiction and protect public health.

The new measures

The Australian government will ban e-cigarettes through a heavy set of controls on imports and packaging to discourage vaping, especially among teens, under its biggest smoking reforms in more than a decade.

Australian Health Minister Mark Butler on Tuesday said vaping has become a top behavioral issue in high schools and a growing problem in elementary schools but recognized the products have a therapeutic use under the right circumstances.

Vaping involves heating a liquid that contains nicotine in an e-cigarette which is vaporized and inhaled by the user. It is widely seen as an alternative to smoking cigarettes and a product to help smokers quit tobacco, but instead, teens and even young children are taking up vaping as an addictive habit globally.

"Vaping was sold to governments and communities around the world as a therapeutic product to help long-term smokers quit," Butler said. "It was not sold as a recreational product – in particular not one for our kids. But that is what it has become – the biggest loophole in Australian history."

Announcing the new regulations, Butler said non-prescription vapes will be banned from importation, and vape products will be required to have pharmaceutical-like packaging, aimed at being sold as products to help smokers quit only. Brightly colored, fun-flavored packs that lured younger users will be restricted, and all single-use and disposable vapes will be banned, Butler added.

"This is a product targeted at our kids, sold alongside lollies and chocolate bars," Butler said. "Just like they did with smoking, let's be clear about this, Big Tobacco has taken another addictive product, wrapped it in shiny packaging and added flavors to create a new generation of nicotine addicts."

Before the changes were announced Tuesday, the only legal way to sell a nicotine vape in Australia was through a prescription provided by a doctor to a pharmacy – but the products were still widely sold across the country. A "black market" of convenience stores and gas stations selling the nicotine vapes without any labeling or warnings to minors have thrived under a lack of regulation and action, according to Butler.

"No more bubblegum flavors. No more pink unicorns. No more vapes deliberately disguised as highlighter pens for kids to be able to hide them in their pencil cases," the health minister added.

Nearly $20 million will be used to help Australians quit vaping and over $41 million earmarked for a national information campaign aimed at youths, Butler said. Australia's tobacco tax will also be increased by 5% per year over the next three years starting on September 1.

The rationale behind the crackdown

The government's decision to ban recreational vaping and tighten other aspects of e-cigarette laws was based on evidence of the potential harms of vaping and the alarming rise in teenage vaping.

According to the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW), the proportion of 12-17 year olds who had ever used an e-cigarette increased from 11% in 2016 to 18% in 2019. The proportion who had used an e-cigarette in the past month increased from 4% to 7% over the same period.

The AIHW also found that among young people who had never smoked tobacco, those who had used an e-cigarette were more likely to intend to smoke tobacco in the future than those who had not used an e-cigarette.

Vaping has been linked to various health risks, such as lung damage, cardiovascular disease, cancer, nicotine addiction, poisoning and injuries from exploding devices. Vaping can also expose users and bystanders to harmful chemicals and metals.

The World Health Organization (WHO) has warned that there is insufficient evidence to support claims that e-cigarettes are effective for smoking cessation and that they may undermine tobacco control efforts by renormalizing smoking behavior.

The WHO has also urged countries to regulate e-cigarettes to protect public health and prevent youth initiation.

The reaction from stakeholders

The government's announcement was welcomed by health groups and anti-smoking advocates, who praised it as a bold and necessary move to protect Australians from the harms of vaping.

The Australian Medical Association (AMA) said it strongly supported the ban on recreational vaping and the new regulations on nicotine products.

"The AMA has long called for stronger action on e-cigarettes because we know they are harmful – especially for young people – and we know they are not proven as an effective smoking cessation aid," AMA president Dr Omar Khorshid said in a statement.

"We also know that e-cigarettes are a gateway to tobacco smoking for many young people who would otherwise never take up smoking."

The Cancer Council Australia said the reforms were "a huge win for public health" and would prevent a new generation of nicotine addicts.

"We commend the government for taking this important step to protect young Australians from being lured into using these highly addictive products by Big Tobacco," Cancer Council Australia CEO Professor Sanchia Aranda said in a statement.

"Vaping is not harmless – it can cause lung damage, cardiovascular disease and cancer, and it can expose users and bystanders to harmful chemicals and metals."

However, not everyone was happy with the government's decision. Some vaping advocates and users argued that the ban on recreational vaping was unfair and would force them back to smoking cigarettes.

The Australian Tobacco Harm Reduction Association (ATHRA), a group that promotes vaping as a safer alternative to smoking, said the reforms were "a disaster for public health" and would deny smokers access to a life-saving tool.

"Vaping is at least 95% less harmful than smoking and has helped millions of smokers quit worldwide," ATHRA chairman Dr Colin Mendelsohn said in a statement.

"The government has ignored the overwhelming scientific evidence and the advice of international experts who support vaping as a harm reduction strategy."

Mendelsohn said the new regulations would create unnecessary barriers for smokers who want to switch to vaping, such as requiring a prescription, limiting nicotine levels and banning flavours.

He also said the ban on non-nicotine vapes was "illogical" and "unjustified", as they do not contain any addictive or harmful substances.

Some vape users also expressed their frustration and disappointment with the government's announcement on social media, saying they felt betrayed and discriminated against.

"I have been vaping for over 5 years now and it has helped me quit smoking cigarettes completely. I don't want to go back to smoking, but I don't want to go through the hassle of getting a prescription either," one user wrote on Twitter.

"This is ridiculous. Vaping is not a crime. It is a personal choice that harms no one. Why should I be treated like a criminal for trying to improve my health?" another user wrote on Facebook.

The global context

Australia is not the only country that has taken action to regulate e-cigarettes and curb youth vaping. Many countries around the world have introduced various measures to control the availability, accessibility and attractiveness of vaping products, such as bans, taxes, age restrictions, packaging requirements and flavour limitations.

According to the WHO, as of July 2020, 32 countries have banned the sale of e-cigarettes, while 79 countries have adopted partial or complete bans on e-cigarette advertising, promotion and sponsorship.

Some countries have also taken steps to address the emergence of new vaping products, such as heated tobacco products (HTPs) and disposable electronic nicotine delivery systems (ENDS), which pose similar health risks and challenges as e-cigarettes.

For example, in February 2020, the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issued a policy that prioritized enforcement against certain unauthorized flavoured e-cigarette products that appeal to kids, including fruit and mint flavours. The policy also targeted unauthorized HTPs and disposable ENDS.

In April 2020, Canada announced new regulations that would limit the nicotine concentration in vaping products to 20 mg/ml, prohibit most flavours in HTPs, require child-resistant packaging for vaping products containing nicotine, and restrict certain types of promotions for vaping products.

In May 2020, New Zealand passed legislation that would prohibit vaping in smokefree areas, restrict sales to people aged 18 years and over, require product safety standards, limit flavours in general retail outlets, and ban advertising of vaping products except at point of sale.

In June 2020, Singapore amended its law to prohibit the importation, distribution and sale of emerging tobacco products such as HTPs and oral tobacco products. The ban will also cover emerging tobacco products that are not yet available in Singapore, such as tobacco-free shisha and smokeless tobacco products.

The government said the ban was necessary to prevent these products from gaining a foothold in the Singapore market and posing health risks to the population.

The conclusion

The Australian government's ban on recreational vaping and new regulations on nicotine products are part of its ongoing efforts to reduce tobacco consumption and protect public health.

The reforms are based on evidence of the potential harms of vaping and the alarming rise in teenage vaping, as well as the recommendations of international health authorities.

The reforms are also in line with global trends, as many countries have taken action to regulate e-cigarettes and curb youth vaping.

The government's announcement has received support from health groups and anti-smoking advocates, who see it as a bold and necessary move to protect Australians from the harms of vaping.

However, some vaping advocates and users have criticized the government's decision, arguing that it is unfair and will force them back to smoking cigarettes.

They have also questioned the evidence and rationale behind the reforms, and called for more consultation and dialogue with the vaping community.

The hazards of e-cigarettes to the human body

E-cigarettes are devices that heat a liquid that contains nicotine or other substances and produce an aerosol that users inhale. E-cigarettes are often marketed as a safer alternative to smoking tobacco, but they are not harmless and can pose serious health risks to the human body.

Some of the hazards of e-cigarettes to the human body include:

  • Nicotine addiction: Nicotine is a highly addictive substance that can affect the brain development of young people and harm the reproductive health of adults. Nicotine can also increase the risk of cardiovascular disease, stroke, and diabetes.
  • Lung damage: E-cigarette aerosol can contain harmful chemicals, such as diacetyl, volatile organic compounds, and heavy metals, that can damage the lungs and cause inflammation, irritation, and respiratory infections. E-cigarette use can also impair the lung function and increase the risk of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, and lung cancer.
  • Heart damage: E-cigarette use can affect the blood vessels and the heart by increasing blood pressure, heart rate, and arterial stiffness. E-cigarette use can also increase the risk of blood clots, heart attack, and heart failure.
  • Cancer: E-cigarette aerosol can contain carcinogens, such as formaldehyde, acetaldehyde, and nitrosamines, that can damage the DNA and cause mutations. E-cigarette use can increase the risk of cancer in various organs, such as the mouth, throat, esophagus, stomach, pancreas, liver, bladder, and cervix.
  • Other effects: E-cigarette use can also have other negative effects on the human body, such as weakening the immune system, causing skin problems, affecting oral health, impairing wound healing, and increasing the risk of poisoning and injuries from exploding devices.

E-cigarettes are not safe for anyone, especially for young people, pregnant women, and people who do not smoke tobacco. The long-term effects of e-cigarettes are still unknown and may be worse than expected. The best way to protect one's health is to avoid e-cigarettes and other tobacco products altogether.
