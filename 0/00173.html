What is stiff person syndrome?

Stiff person syndrome (SPS) is a rare disorder that causes progressive muscle stiffness and painful spasms, mainly in the trunk and limbs. The spasms can be triggered by sudden movements, noises, emotional stress, or infections. The condition can also affect breathing, posture, balance, and mobility. Some people with SPS may have other symptoms such as double vision, slurred speech, anxiety, or depression.

SPS is thought to be an autoimmune disease, meaning that the body's own immune system mistakenly attacks healthy cells and tissues. In SPS, the immune system produces antibodies that target a protein called glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD), which is involved in making a chemical messenger called gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA). GABA helps regulate muscle tone and movement by inhibiting nerve signals. When GAD is attacked by antibodies, GABA production is reduced, leading to increased nerve activity and muscle rigidity.

SPS affects about one or two people in a million, and is more common in women than men. It usually develops between the ages of 40 and 50, but can occur at any age. The exact cause of SPS is unknown, but some factors that may increase the risk include having other autoimmune diseases (such as type 1 diabetes or thyroid disorders), having certain genetic variations, or having a history of viral infections.

How is stiff person syndrome diagnosed and treated?

Diagnosing SPS can be challenging because the symptoms can vary widely and mimic other conditions. There is no specific test for SPS, but doctors may use a combination of methods to rule out other causes and confirm the diagnosis. These methods may include:

  • Medical history and physical examination: The doctor will ask about the onset, duration, frequency, and severity of the symptoms, as well as any personal or family history of autoimmune diseases. The doctor will also check for signs of muscle stiffness, spasms, reflexes, and coordination.
  • Blood tests: The doctor will look for the presence of GAD antibodies in the blood, which are found in about 60% to 80% of people with SPS. However, some people with SPS may not have GAD antibodies, and some people without SPS may have them. Therefore, blood tests alone are not enough to diagnose SPS.
  • Electromyography (EMG): This test measures the electrical activity of the muscles at rest and during contraction. In SPS, EMG may show continuous muscle activity even when the muscles are relaxed.
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): This test uses a strong magnetic field and radio waves to create detailed images of the brain and spinal cord. MRI can help rule out other neurological disorders that may cause similar symptoms.

There is no cure for SPS, but treatments can help manage the symptoms and improve the quality of life. The main goals of treatment are to reduce muscle stiffness and spasms, prevent complications such as falls or fractures, and address any underlying autoimmune conditions. Treatment options may include:

  • Medications: Several types of drugs can help relax the muscles and reduce nerve activity. These include benzodiazepines (such as diazepam or clonazepam), baclofen (a muscle relaxant), anticonvulsants (such as gabapentin or pregabalin), immunosuppressants (such as azathioprine or rituximab), or intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG), which contains antibodies from healthy donors that can block the harmful effects of GAD antibodies.
  • Physical therapy: A physical therapist can design an exercise program to improve muscle strength, flexibility, posture, balance, and mobility. Physical therapy can also help prevent muscle atrophy (wasting) and contractures (shortening) due to prolonged stiffness.
  • Occupational therapy: An occupational therapist can help with daily activities such as dressing, bathing, eating, or working. Occupational therapy can also provide assistive devices such as braces, splints, walkers, or wheelchairs to improve function and safety.
  • Psychotherapy: A psychotherapist can help with coping strategies for stress management, anxiety reduction, depression prevention, and emotional support. Psychotherapy can also address any social isolation or stigma that may result from having SPS.
