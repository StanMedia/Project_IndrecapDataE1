US and Philippines show off firepower in South China Sea drills

Defence-Imagery/Pixabay

The two allies conducted their first live-fire exercise at sea as part of their largest annual war games, amid rising tensions with China.

The exercise involved air, land and sea assets from both countries

The United States and the Philippines fired on a mock enemy warship in the South China Sea on Wednesday, in a display of their military might and cooperation in the face of China's growing assertiveness in the disputed waters.

The exercise, which was witnessed by Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr, was part of the Balikatan (shoulder-to-shoulder) drills, the largest annual war games between the two treaty allies, involving more than 17,000 troops.

US aircraft, including F-35 and F-16 fighter jets, as well as HIMARS rocket systems and Cobra helicopters joined with Philippine FA-50 fighter jets, helicopters and artillery to fire on a decommissioned warship towed to a site within Philippine territorial waters off the island of Luzon.

Luzon, the northernmost of the Philippines main islands, is only 280 miles (452 kilometers) from Taiwan, the self-ruled island over which China claims sovereignty despite never having ruled it.

The exercise was the first time that the Balikatan drills featured live-fire drills at sea, at a location just a few hours by boat from one of the world's most contested maritime features, the Scarborough Shoal, which China seized from the Philippines in 2012.

The exercise was aimed at enhancing interoperability and readiness

The US and Philippine military leaders said the exercise was designed to enhance interoperability and readiness of their forces in full-spectrum military operations across all domains.

"This training increased the exercise's realism and complexity, a key priority shared between the Armed Forces of the Philippines and the US military," said Lt. Gen. William Jurney, the commander of US Marine Corps Forces, Pacific, in a statement.

"Together we are strengthening our capabilities in full-spectrum military operations across all domains," Jurney said.

Philippine Defense Secretary Delfin Lorenzana said the exercise demonstrated the strong bond and mutual trust between the two allies.

"This is a testament to our unwavering commitment to defend our sovereignty and uphold our shared values," Lorenzana said.

Marcos, who was joined by his defense and armed forces chiefs, boarded and inspected a HIMARS cab displayed at the San Antonio naval base before observing the exercise through binoculars and on a TV screen.

He smiled when the first missile was fired, leaving a trail of smoke in the sky.

The exercise came amid rising tensions with China in the region

The Balikatan exercises come amid warming relations between the US and the Philippines following the election of Marcos Jr, who has sought to develop closer ties with Washington in contrast to his predecessor Rodrigo Duterte, who tilted toward China.

The US and the Philippines have maintained a mutual defense treaty since 1951, but under Duterte the Philippines downplayed longstanding territorial disputes with Beijing while seeking to attract investment.

The Balikatan exercises also follow the announcement that the Philippines will grant increased access by US forces to bases in the archipelago under the 2014 Enhanced Defense Cooperation Arrangement (EDCA).

The four bases joined five included earlier, including three on Luzon close to Taiwan, and one on Balabac Island close to Chinese installations on disputed islands in the South China Sea.

China has criticized the military exercises, which come against the backdrop of what the Philippines calls "aggressive" Chinese actions in the South China Sea.

Beijing has said that it has indisputable sovereignty over most of the South China Sea and has built artificial islands with military facilities on some of them. It has also accused the US of meddling in regional affairs and militarizing the waters.

The US has said that it does not take sides in territorial disputes but supports freedom of navigation and overflight in accordance with international law. It has also conducted regular naval and aerial patrols to challenge China's claims and reassure its allies and partners.
