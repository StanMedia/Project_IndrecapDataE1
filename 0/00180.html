Why lutein is not enough for eye health and how to get a balanced diet for your eyes

Bruno Henrique/pexels

Lutein is a popular supplement for eye health, but it is not the only nutrient that your eyes need. Here are some other important nutrients and foods that can protect your vision and prevent eye diseases.

What is lutein and how does it benefit the eyes?

Lutein is a type of carotenoid, which is a pigment that gives color to fruits and vegetables. Lutein is mainly found in green leafy vegetables, such as kale, spinach, and broccoli. Lutein can also be found in egg yolks, corn, and some fruits.

Lutein has antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties that can protect the eyes from oxidative stress and inflammation. Lutein is especially concentrated in the macula, which is the central part of the retina that is responsible for sharp and detailed vision. Lutein can filter out harmful blue light and prevent damage to the macula.

Studies have shown that lutein can lower the risk of age-related macular degeneration (AMD), which is a leading cause of blindness in older adults. Lutein can also improve visual acuity, contrast sensitivity, and glare recovery. Lutein may also help prevent or delay cataracts, which are clouding of the lens of the eye.

What are some other nutrients and foods that are good for the eyes?

Lutein is not the only nutrient that can benefit the eyes. There are other nutrients and foods that can also support eye health and prevent eye diseases. Some of them are:

  • Zeaxanthin: Zeaxanthin is another type of carotenoid that works together with lutein to protect the macula. Zeaxanthin is found in similar foods as lutein, such as green leafy vegetables, eggs, corn, and some fruits.
  • Vitamin A: Vitamin A is essential for the production of rhodopsin, which is a pigment that helps the eyes see in dim light. Vitamin A also helps maintain the health of the cornea, which is the outer layer of the eye. Vitamin A deficiency can cause night blindness, dry eyes, and increased susceptibility to infections. Vitamin A is found in animal sources, such as liver, fish oil, eggs, and dairy products. It can also be converted from beta-carotene, which is a precursor of vitamin A found in plant sources, such as carrots, sweet potatoes, pumpkin, and mangoes.
  • Vitamin C: Vitamin C is a powerful antioxidant that can protect the eyes from free radical damage and inflammation. Vitamin C also helps synthesize collagen, which is a protein that provides structure and strength to the eye tissues. Vitamin C deficiency can lead to scurvy, which can cause bleeding gums, poor wound healing, and increased risk of infections. Vitamin C is found in citrus fruits, such as oranges, grapefruits, lemons, and limes. It can also be found in other fruits and vegetables, such as strawberries, kiwis, bell peppers, broccoli, and tomatoes.
  • Vitamin E: Vitamin E is another antioxidant that can protect the eyes from oxidative stress and inflammation. Vitamin E also helps prevent lipid peroxidation, which is a process that damages the cell membranes of the eye tissues. Vitamin E deficiency can cause hemolytic anemia, which can impair oxygen delivery to the eyes. Vitamin E is found in nuts and seeds, such as almonds, sunflower seeds, and hazelnuts. It can also be found in vegetable oils, such as sunflower oil, safflower oil, and wheat germ oil.
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: Omega-3 fatty acids are essential fats that have anti-inflammatory effects and help maintain the fluidity of cell membranes. Omega-3 fatty acids are especially important for the health of the retina, which contains a high concentration of these fats. Omega-3 fatty acids may help prevent or treat dry eye syndrome, glaucoma, and AMD. Omega-3 fatty acids are found in oily fish, such as salmon, mackerel, herring, and sardines. They can also be found in flaxseeds, chia seeds, walnuts, and soybeans.
  • Zinc: Zinc is a mineral that plays a vital role in many enzymatic reactions and cellular processes in the eye. Zinc helps transport vitamin A from the liver to the retina, where it is used to produce melanin, a protective pigment. Zinc also helps protect the eyes from oxidative stress and inflammation. Zinc deficiency can cause night blindness, poor wound healing, and increased susceptibility to infections. Zinc is found in animal sources, such as oysters, beef, pork, and chicken. It can also be found in plant sources, such as beans, lentils, nuts, and seeds.

How much of these nutrients do you need for eye health?

The recommended dietary allowances (RDAs) or adequate intakes (AIs) for these nutrients vary depending on your age, sex, and health status. Here are some general guidelines for adults:

  • Lutein and zeaxanthin: There is no official RDA or AI for these carotenoids, but some studies suggest that 6–10 mg per day may be beneficial for eye health (12).
  • Vitamin A: The RDA for vitamin A is 900 mcg for men and 700 mcg for women. However, pregnant and lactating women may need more (13).
  • Vitamin C: The RDA for vitamin C is 90 mg for men and 75 mg for women. However, smokers may need an extra 35 mg per day (14).
  • Vitamin E: The RDA for vitamin E is 15 mg for both men and women. However, people with certain medical conditions may need more or less (15).
  • Omega-3 fatty acids: There is no official RDA or AI for omega-3 fatty acids, but the World Health Organization (WHO) recommends consuming at least 250–500 mg of combined EPA and DHA per day. ALA, another type of omega-3 fatty acid, can be converted into EPA and DHA in the body, but the conversion rate is low (16).
  • Zinc: The RDA for zinc is 11 mg for men and 8 mg for women. However, pregnant and lactating women may need more (17).

It is possible to get enough of these nutrients from a balanced diet that includes a variety of fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, legumes, whole grains, fish, eggs, and dairy products. However, some people may benefit from taking supplements if they have a deficiency or a higher risk of eye diseases.

Before taking any supplements, consult your doctor or a registered dietitian to determine the appropriate dosage and potential interactions with medications or other supplements.

Summary

Lutein is a carotenoid that can protect the macula from blue light damage and lower the risk of AMD and cataracts. However, lutein alone is not enough for eye health. Other nutrients, such as zeaxanthin, vitamin A, vitamin C, vitamin E, omega-3 fatty acids, and zinc, are also important for maintaining eye function and preventing eye diseases. A balanced diet that includes a variety of colorful fruits and vegetables, nuts and seeds, legumes, whole grains, fish, eggs, and dairy products can provide these nutrients. Some people may also benefit from taking supplements under medical supervision.
