How new grads can manage their money wisely

Experts share their advice on how to budget, save, invest and pay off debt after graduating from college.

Why budgeting is the first step

Budgeting is the process of planning how to spend your income and allocating it to different categories, such as needs, wants and savings. Budgeting can help you live within your means, avoid overspending and achieve your financial goals.

One simple way to create a budget is to use the 50/30/20 rule. This means spending 50% of your income on needs, such as rent, groceries and minimum loan payments; 30% on wants, such as travel, entertainment and dining out; and 20% on savings and extra payments on high-interest debt.

You can use a budgeting app or spreadsheet to track your income and expenses and see how much you have left for each category. You can also adjust your budget as your income or expenses change over time.

How to prioritize your money goals

You may have multiple money goals, such as saving for emergencies, retirement or a down payment; paying off student loans or credit cards; or investing in the stock market. However, you can't do everything at once, so you need to prioritize your goals based on their urgency and importance.

Here is a suggested order of priority for your money goals:

  • Save $500 for emergencies in a high-yield savings account. This will help you cover unexpected expenses without relying on credit cards or loans.
  • Contribute enough to your 401(k) or other employer-sponsored retirement plan to get the full match, if there is one. This is free money that will grow over time due to compound interest.
  • Pay off high-interest debt, such as credit cards or payday loans. This will save you money on interest and free up cash flow for other goals.
  • Save for retirement in an individual retirement account (IRA) or a Roth IRA. Aim for 15% of your pre-tax income. The sooner you start saving for retirement, the more time your money has to grow.
  • Grow your emergency fund to cover three to six months' worth of expenses. This will give you more financial security and peace of mind in case of a job loss, medical emergency or other crisis.
  • Save for other short-term or long-term goals, such as buying a car, traveling or starting a business. You can use a separate savings account or an investment account for each goal.

How to understand investing basics

Investing is the process of putting your money into assets that can generate income or appreciate in value over time, such as stocks, bonds, mutual funds or real estate. Investing can help you grow your wealth and reach your financial goals faster than saving alone.

However, investing also involves risk and requires knowledge and discipline. Here are some investing basics you should know:

  • Diversify your portfolio. This means spreading your money across different types of assets and industries to reduce your exposure to any single risk factor.
  • Invest for the long term. This means holding your investments for at least five years or longer and avoiding frequent buying and selling based on market fluctuations.
  • Use low-cost index funds or exchange-traded funds (ETFs). These are funds that track the performance of a broad market index, such as the S&P 500 or the Nasdaq 100. They offer diversification, low fees and consistent returns.
  • Take advantage of tax-advantaged accounts. These are accounts that offer tax benefits for investing, such as 401(k)s, IRAs and Roth IRAs. They can help you save more money for retirement and reduce your tax bill.

How to deal with student debt

Student debt is one of the biggest financial challenges facing new grads. According to the latest data from the Federal Reserve Bank of New York, the average student debt balance per borrower was $38,792 in the third quarter of 2022.

Here are some steps you can take to manage your student debt:

  • Know what you owe. Find out how much you owe in total, what types of loans you have (federal or private), what interest rates you pay and what repayment plans you are on.
  • Make a repayment plan. Choose a repayment plan that fits your budget and goals. For federal loans, you may qualify for income-driven repayment plans that cap your monthly payments at a percentage of your income and offer loan forgiveness after 20 or 25 years. For private loans, you may be able to refinance them at a lower interest rate if you have good credit and income.
  • Pay more than the minimum. If you can afford it, pay more than the minimum amount due each month on your highest-interest loans. This will help you pay off your debt faster and save money on interest.
  • Seek forgiveness or assistance programs. Depending on your profession or situation, you may be eligible for student loan forgiveness or assistance programs that can reduce or eliminate some of your debt. For example, teachers, nurses, public servants and military personnel may qualify for certain programs.

How to enjoy life without breaking the bank

While managing your money wisely is important, it doesn't mean you have to deprive yourself of fun and happiness. You can still enjoy life without breaking the bank by following these tips:

  • Set aside money for splurges. As part of your budget, allocate some money for things that bring you joy, such as hobbies, experiences or treats. This will help you avoid feeling deprived or guilty about spending money on yourself.
  • Look for ways to save money on things you love. You don't have to spend a lot of money to have fun. You can look for discounts, coupons, deals or free events that offer entertainment or value. You can also use cash-back apps or credit cards that reward you for spending on things you love.
  • Find balance between spending and saving. While it's good to save money for the future, it's also important to live in the present and enjoy what you have now. Find a balance between spending and saving that works for you and aligns with your values and priorities.
