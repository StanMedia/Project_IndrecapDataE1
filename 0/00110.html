How a thunderstorm can produce a tornado: A scientific explanation

Ralph W. lambrecht/pexels

A tornado is one of the most violent and destructive natural phenomena on Earth. But how does it form from a thunderstorm? Here is a scientific explanation of the process.

The development of a thunderstorm

A thunderstorm is a type of weather system that produces heavy rain, lightning, hail and strong winds. It forms when warm, moist air rises rapidly in the atmosphere, creating instability and convection. As the air rises, it cools and condenses into clouds that grow taller and taller. The clouds can reach heights of up to 20 kilometers (12 miles) or more, forming an anvil shape at the top.

The formation of a supercell

A supercell is a special kind of thunderstorm that has a rotating updraft, or a column of rising air that spins like a top. The rotation is caused by wind shear, which is a change in wind speed and direction with height. Wind shear can tilt the updraft and create a horizontal vortex of air that stretches along the storm. This vortex can then be turned vertically by the updraft, forming a mesocyclone, or a rotating storm cloud.

A supercell is the most powerful and long-lived type of thunderstorm, capable of producing severe weather such as large hail, damaging winds and tornadoes. However, not all supercells produce tornadoes, and not all tornadoes come from supercells. Scientists are still studying the factors that determine whether a supercell will spawn a tornado or not.

The formation of a tornado

A tornado is a violently rotating column of air that extends from a thunderstorm cloud to the ground. It is usually visible as a funnel-shaped cloud or a dark cloud of debris and dust. A tornado can have wind speeds of up to 500 kilometers per hour (300 miles per hour) or more, making it the strongest wind phenomenon on Earth.

A tornado usually forms when there is spinning air near the ground, which can be caused by gusts of warm and cold air blowing across the land. This spinning air can be drawn into the updraft of the supercell and stretched vertically, increasing its speed and creating a vortex. This vortex can then be wrapped by rain and hail in the storm, forming a wall cloud, which is a lowering of the cloud base near the center of the mesocyclone.

The wall cloud can then produce one or more smaller vortices that extend downward from it, forming tornadoes. These vortices are usually invisible until they pick up dust and debris from the ground or condense water vapor into clouds. The most intense tornadoes are those that remain in contact with both the updraft and the downdraft of the supercell, which provide them with energy and stability.

The types and impacts of tornadoes

Tornadoes can vary in size, shape, color and intensity. They can be classified by their appearance, such as rope-like, cone-shaped or wedge-shaped; by their direction of movement, such as clockwise or counterclockwise; or by their strength, using the Enhanced Fujita Scale (EF-Scale), which ranges from EF0 to EF5 based on the damage they cause.

Tornadoes can cause devastating impacts on people, animals, buildings and infrastructure. They can destroy houses, uproot trees, hurl vehicles and objects into the air, and injure or kill anyone in their path. They can also trigger other hazards such as flash floods, landslides and fires.

Tornadoes are most common in regions where warm and moist air meets cold and dry air, such as in the central and southern United States (known as Tornado Alley), parts of Europe, Asia and Australia. However, they can occur anywhere in the world at any time of year, especially during spring and summer when thunderstorms are more frequent.

Tornadoes are difficult to predict and track, as they can form quickly and change direction unpredictably. Therefore, it is important to be aware of the signs of a possible tornado, such as dark clouds, strong winds, hail, loud noises or flying debris; and to follow safety precautions such as seeking shelter in a sturdy building or underground; avoiding windows, doors and cars; covering your head with your arms or an object; and listening to weather alerts and warnings.
